Hoya Plant Care Guide

Hoyas come in a huge variety, are easy to care for, bloom, don't need frequent repotting, are non-toxic to pets, and are generally compact. They're often called wax plants because of their thick, waxy leaves — though many species have thinner, softer foliage.

Care tips adapted from Hoya Treasures

Soil & Medium

Many growers use a DIY pon mix (semi-hydroponic) or a chunky, well-draining aroid mix. Pon works especially well in self-watering systems.

  • ✓ Pumice, perlite, leca, or pon for drainage
  • ✓ Orchid bark + perlite + potting soil works great
  • ✓ Hoyas love being root-bound — don't overpot

Watering

Water when the pot feels lighter than when it's wet. In pon, you can visually see when it's dry and chalky.

  • ✓ Self-watering pots with wicking systems work best with pon
  • ✓ Without self-watering, water every other day or mist daily
  • ✓ Roots should not sit directly in standing water

Fertilizer

Fertilize lightly with each watering. Rotate fertilizers for balanced nutrition, then flush every fourth week.

Wk 1 Super Thrive Foliage Pro — ½ tsp

New Plant Acclimation

When you receive a new hoya, follow these steps to give it the best start:

1

Unbox & Inspect

Check for pests or damage. Give it a little water and light after days in a dark box.

2

Quarantine 3 Weeks

Keep away from your collection. Don't repot, trim leaves, or cut roots during this period.

3

Don't Stress

A few broken or yellowing leaves are 100% normal from shipping stress. They'll recover.

4

Repot (Optional)

After 3 weeks, move to your preferred mix. Keep the same pot size — only go up 1″ when root-bound.

Pest Prevention & Treatment

Spray all plants weekly for pests — whether you see signs of them or not. Prevention is far easier than treatment.

Preventive (Weekly)

PureCrop — 4 Tbsp per gallon of water. Targets root mealies. Adjust pH (it raises pH significantly). Leave lights on while drying.
Horticultural Oil — 2 tsp per gallon of water. Apply after PureCrop dries. Turn lights off overnight.
Kontos (systemic) — ¼ tsp per gallon, every 3 weeks. Takes ~3 weeks to activate. Good long-term preventative.

Active Infestation

BioAdvanced Complete Insect Killer for Soil & Turf — 1 Tbsp per gallon of water. Kills at all life-cycle stages.
Safety: Wear mask & gloves. Spray outside if possible. Toxic to inhale. Do not use on outdoor flowering plants — harmful to pollinators.

How to Get Your Hoya to Bloom

Getting flowers is the #1 thing Hoya growers ask about. Most plants won't bloom until they're mature, and light is the biggest lever. Dial these in and you'll be rewarded with umbels of waxy, often fragrant flowers.

1 · Maturity first
Most Hoyas flower only once mature (often 2–3+ years) and bloom better slightly pot-bound. A young cutting won't bloom yet — be patient.
2 · Maximize light
The single biggest trigger. Bright indirect light or a few hours of gentle sun. Too little light is the most common reason a healthy Hoya refuses to bloom; grow lights help.
3 · Never cut the spurs
Flowers form on peduncles (spurs) that rebloom from the same point for years. Snipping them off removes every future bloom from that spur.
4 · Don't move a budding plant
Once buds appear, leave it put. Rotating or relocating can cause “bud blast” — buds shrivel and drop before opening.
5 · A little stress helps
Slightly rootbound, a touch drier between waterings, and cooler autumn nights can all nudge a mature plant into bud.
6 · Feed for flowers
Consistent light feeding through the growing season; a higher-phosphorus “bloom” feed as days lengthen can encourage budding.
Good to know: sticky droplets on or beneath the flowers are sweet nectar — completely normal (it's why they're called wax / honey plants), not a sign of pests.

Common Problems & Fixes

Yellowing leaves

Most often overwatering or a soggy, poorly-draining mix — make sure roots aren't sitting wet and that the medium dries out between waterings. A single old leaf yellowing low on the vine is just natural aging.

Wrinkled, shriveled leaves

Two opposite causes — check the roots to tell them apart. Firm white roots = simply thirsty, so water it. Mushy brown roots = rot blocking water uptake: cut away the rot, repot into fresh dry mix, and rehydrate slowly.

Dropping leaves

Usually a cold draft, a sudden change in environment, or chronic overwatering. Keep plants above 60°F and away from AC and heater vents.

No flowers

Almost always not enough light or a plant that isn't mature yet — and sometimes spurs that were accidentally cut off. Work through the bloom guide above; more light is the usual fix.

Sticky leaves or black sooty film

If the plant isn't blooming, suspect sap-sucking pests (mealybugs, scale) leaving honeydew that sooty mold grows on — treat per the pest section. If it is in bloom, it's just harmless flower nectar.

Buds drying and dropping (bud blast)

The plant was moved, allowed to dry too far, or humidity crashed while it was setting buds. Hold light, water, and humidity steady once buds appear and don't rotate the pot.

Crispy brown leaf edges

Too much direct sun, water run too low, or fertilizer salt buildup. Flush the pot with plain water, ease off harsh direct light, and keep watering more consistent.

Quick Tips for Faster Growth

Keep them root-bound
Hoyas bloom and grow better when snug. Only go up 1″ when roots push all sides.
Humidity 60–73%
Higher humidity = faster growth. Use a humidifier or group plants together.
Bright indirect light
More light = more growth & blooms. Grow lights work great for supplementing.
Fertilize consistently
A little with each watering beats heavy monthly doses. Rotate products.
Self-watering pots
Wicking systems with pon keep moisture perfectly consistent.
Weekly pest spray
Prevention is everything. Spray whether you see pests or not.

Step-by-Step: Water Propagation

The foolproof method for 90% of Hoyas — and the most satisfying to watch.

  1. 1

    Choose a healthy cutting

    Pick a vine with at least 2–3 nodes (the bumps where leaves attach). Avoid flowering spurs — leave those intact so your mother plant can rebloom. Use clean, sharp snips and cut about ½" below a node.

  2. 2

    Strip the lower leaves

    Remove leaves from the bottom 1–2 nodes. These nodes are where roots will emerge — leaves submerged in water will rot and cloud everything.

  3. 3

    Callous (optional but recommended)

    Let the fresh cut air-dry for 1–2 hours. This seals the wound and dramatically reduces rot, especially for thick-stemmed species like H. kerrii and H. pachyclada.

  4. 4

    Submerge the nodes

    Place in room-temperature filtered or rainwater with bare nodes fully submerged and leaves above the water line. A clear glass jar lets you monitor progress.

  5. 5

    Warmth + bright indirect light

    Target 70–80°F. A heat mat speeds things up noticeably. Bright indirect light — never direct sun, which cooks roots in water.

  6. 6

    Refresh water weekly

    Swap water every 5–7 days to keep oxygen levels up and prevent stagnation. If it turns cloudy, change sooner and give the cutting a quick rinse.

  7. 7

    Wait for 1–2" of roots

    Don't pot up too early. You want roots with visible fine hairs and multiple branches — usually 2–6 weeks in.

  8. 8

    Transition to soil gradually

    Water roots are structurally different from soil roots. Pot into a chunky, airy mix (orchid bark + perlite + a little coco coir), keep evenly moist for the first 2 weeks, then resume normal Hoya care.

Do

  • ✓Use cuttings with 2+ nodes — single-leaf cuttings rarely vine.
  • ✓Propagate in spring or summer during active growth.
  • ✓Keep humidity 60–80% (a plastic bag or prop box works).
  • ✓Use bottom heat (heat mat) — it halves rooting time.
  • ✓Label with date + species so you can track what's working.

Don't

  • ✗Cut through or remove flowering peduncles — Hoyas rebloom on the same spur for years.
  • ✗Propagate single heart leaves of H. kerrii and expect a vine (they almost never grow).
  • ✗Use dense potting soil — Hoyas are epiphytes and will rot in it.
  • ✗Unpot freshly rooted cuttings to "check" progress — disturbance stalls them.
  • ✗Over-fertilize new roots — wait 4+ weeks after potting up.

Troubleshooting

Cutting is mushy or blackening

Rot, usually from a pathogen entering the cut or from cold water. Trim back above the damage until you hit clean green tissue, dust with cinnamon (a natural antifungal), let it callous for a few hours, then restart in fresh water.

No roots after 6+ weeks

Most likely too cold. Hoyas stall below 65°F. Move the prop to a warmer spot or add a heat mat. Also check that at least one node is actually submerged or in contact with moist medium.

Leaves wrinkling on the cutting

Normal — the cutting is drawing on leaf reserves while rooting. Boost humidity (loose plastic bag over the setup) and it'll recover once roots establish.

Roots in water but declining after potting

Water roots shocked by soil. Keep the mix lightly moist (not wet) for 2 weeks, raise humidity, and avoid fertilizer. Some water roots will die back and be replaced by new soil-adapted ones — this is normal.

Algae in the propagation jar

Harmless but a sign light is too strong. Move to slightly less bright spot, switch to an opaque container, or wrap the jar in paper.
