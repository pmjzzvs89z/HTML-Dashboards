Growing Michigan Native Wildflowers

Natives are easy if you match each plant to the spot it already wants. Get the light and moisture right and most ask for almost nothing — no fertilizer, little water, and years of bloom that feed Michigan's bees, butterflies and birds.

Right Plant, Right Place

The whole secret to low-maintenance natives. Match the plant to your site instead of fighting it.

  • ✓ Use the Habitat, Light and Water filters to find species that fit your spot
  • ✓ Woodland species for shade; prairie species for hot, open sun
  • ✓ A happy native shrugs off drought, pests and Michigan winters

Soil & Site

Most natives evolved on lean soil — rich, fertilized beds make them flop.

  • ✓ Skip the fertilizer — it causes weak, floppy growth and fewer flowers
  • ✓ Don't amend to change soil — match a plant to the soil you have
  • ✓ Good drainage for prairie/dune species; keep wetland species damp

Watering

The goal of year one is deep roots. After that, rain usually does the job.

  • ✓ Water deeply and regularly the first growing season
  • ✓ Once established, most dry & prairie species need little to none
  • ✓ Wetland species (cardinal flower, Joe-Pye, lobelias) want steady moisture

Light & Spacing

Light drives bloom; spacing drives airflow and self-sufficiency.

  • ✓ Prairie species want 6+ hours of sun or they stretch and flop
  • ✓ Plant in drifts of 3–7; let them knit together to crowd out weeds
  • ✓ Tall species: pinch asters & bee balm by July for shorter, sturdier plants

Plan for Bloom All Season

Combine species from each window so something is always in flower — and pollinators always have food, from the first spring bees to the last migrating monarchs.

Spring

Woodland ephemerals — bloodroot, bluebells, trillium, spring beauty — bloom and fade before the tree canopy closes. Plant them under deciduous trees.

Summer

The prairie peak — milkweeds, bee balm, coneflowers, black-eyed Susans and blazing stars run hot through the longest days.

Fall

Asters and gentians carry the show to frost, providing the critical last nectar for monarchs heading south.

Planting a Potted Native

1

Plant spring or fall

Cooler, wetter weather lets roots establish. Avoid mid-summer heat unless you can water faithfully.

2

Loosen & set level

Dig twice the width, tease circling roots, and set the crown at soil level — never bury it.

3

Water in & mulch lightly

Soak well, then add 1–2″ of leaf or bark mulch — kept off the crown.

4

Tend year one

Water through the first season and weed around young plants. Many natives "sleep, creep, then leap" by year three.

Feeding Wildlife

Native wildflowers do far more than nectar — many are the only food certain insects can use, and those insects feed the birds.

Host plants matter most
Milkweeds feed monarchs; wild lupine is the sole food of the endangered Karner blue; violets host fritillaries.
Bloom in succession
Overlapping spring-to-fall bloom keeps nectar flowing for bees and migrating butterflies.
Leave the stems & seed heads
Don't cut back in fall — hollow stems shelter native bees and seed heads feed goldfinches all winter.
Go pesticide-free
Insecticides (including many "systemic" treated plants) defeat the entire purpose — skip them.

Common Problems & Fixes

Tall plants flop over

Usually too much shade or too-rich soil. Give prairie species full sun, stop fertilizing, and pinch back asters and bee balm by half in early July for shorter, self-supporting plants.

No flowers the first year

Normal. Many perennials "sleep, creep, then leap" — they build roots for a year or two before blooming well. Black-eyed Susan and coreopsis are exceptions that bloom fast from seed.

One species is taking over

Watch the Spreads tag in the catalog. Give aggressive spreaders (common milkweed, cup plant, mayapple) room to roam, or corral them and pull excess each spring.

A plant vanished by mid-summer

Spring ephemerals (bluebells, trout lily, Dutchman's breeches) naturally go dormant after blooming — they're fine. Mark their spots so you don't dig into them, and let later plants fill the gap.

Powdery white film on leaves

Powdery mildew, common on bee balm and asters in late summer. It's cosmetic — improve airflow with wider spacing, choose mildew-resistant species (smooth aster, wild bergamot), and avoid overhead watering.

Deer or rabbits browsing

Lean on plants they tend to avoid — milkweeds, bee balm, mountain mint, wild geranium and anything aromatic or fuzzy. Protect tender spring growth with fencing until plants establish.

How to Propagate Native Wildflowers

Most Michigan natives are easy and cheap to multiply from seed — the trick is giving the seed the cold, damp winter it expects. Division and self-sowing are even simpler.

Winter Sowing

Easiest

Sow in jugs outdoors and let real winter do the work — no fridge space needed.

Best for

Nearly everything — milkweeds, coneflowers, asters, bergamot, blazing stars.

Timing

Dec–Feb; sprouts in spring

Direct Fall Sowing

Easiest

Scatter seed on prepared ground in fall and let nature stratify it in place.

Best for

Meadows & large areas; species that resent transplanting (lupine, butterfly weed, baptisia).

Timing

Oct–Nov, before snow

Division

Easy

Split a clump into several plants — the fastest way to more blooms.

Best for

Spreading clumpers — bee balm, asters, mountain mint, irises, golden Alexanders.

Timing

Early spring or fall

Let It Self-Sow

Easiest

Do nothing — leave seed heads and let plants colonize on their own.

Best for

Columbine, black-eyed Susan, spiderwort, golden Alexanders, blue vervain.

Timing

Leave seed heads through winter

Step-by-Step: Winter Sowing in a Jug

The foolproof way to give native seed its required cold-moist stratification — outdoors, hands-off.

  1. 1

    Prep a milk jug

    Cut a clean translucent gallon jug around the middle, leaving a hinge. Punch drainage holes in the bottom and a few in the top. Leave the cap off.

  2. 2

    Add moist mix

    Fill the bottom with 3–4″ of damp seed-starting mix.

  3. 3

    Sow the seed

    Scatter seed on top. Press tiny seed in for light contact; barely cover larger seed. As a rule, sow no deeper than the seed is wide.

  4. 4

    Tape & label

    Tape the jug shut, label with species and date (a pencil on tape survives winter), and write on the jug too.

  5. 5

    Set it outside & forget it

    Put jugs in a spot that gets snow, rain and freeze-thaw cycles. Don't bring them in — the cold is the whole point.

  6. 6

    Watch for spring sprouts

    Seedlings appear as it warms. Vent on warm days so they don't cook, and keep the mix damp.

  7. 7

    Harden off & pot up

    Once they have a couple of true leaves, separate the "hunk-of-seedlings" into pots or plugs and grow on.

  8. 8

    Plant out

    Set plugs in the garden in late spring once established. Water through their first season.

Step-by-Step: Dividing a Clump

For established clumping perennials — quick, free plants that bloom the same year.

  1. 1

    Time it right

    Divide in early spring as growth emerges, or in fall 6 weeks before hard frost. Avoid summer heat.

  2. 2

    Water the day before

    A well-hydrated clump lifts cleaner and recovers faster.

  3. 3

    Lift the whole clump

    Dig a generous circle and pry the root ball out with a fork, keeping as many roots as you can.

  4. 4

    Split into pieces

    Pull apart by hand or cut with a sharp spade. Each division needs healthy roots and a few growth buds or shoots.

  5. 5

    Replant immediately

    Set divisions at the original depth, firm the soil, and don't let roots dry out while you work.

  6. 6

    Water & shade briefly

    Soak well and keep evenly moist for a few weeks. Light afternoon shade helps spring divisions settle.

Do

  • ✓Give most native seed a cold-moist period (winter sowing or 30–60 days in the fridge in damp sand).
  • ✓Direct-sow taprooted species (lupine, butterfly weed, baptisia) where they'll stay.
  • ✓Scarify or inoculate hard-coated legume seed (lupine, baptisia) for better germination.
  • ✓Collect seed from your own or local plants — never strip wild populations.

Don't

  • ✗Dig plants from the wild — it's harmful and often illegal (trilliums and lady's slippers especially).
  • ✗Expect spring sowing without cold treatment to work — most seed will just sit.
  • ✗Sow seed too deep — many natives need light to germinate; surface-sow tiny seed.
  • ✗Let seedlings dry out — keep them evenly moist until well rooted.
